\section{Overview: what we are asking, and why it is subtle}
%==============================================================================

\paragraph{The question.}
A frozen, pretrained video model (\vjepa{}) turns a clip into a stack of feature latent vectors.The question is whether those latents encode \emph{physics} --- where an object is, how fast it moves, which way it is heading,
gravity, collisions, whether an object that goes out of sight still ``exists'' --- and, if so, whether
that physics is (i) easy to read out and (ii) something we can actively \emph{change}.

%==============================================================================
\section{Pipeline (what is built and runs)}
\label{sec:pipeline}
%==============================================================================

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Encode.} Run a frozen \vjepa{}-large encoder (ViT-L, 24 layers, hidden size 1024) over each clip.
\item \textbf{Cache.} Write the multi-layer token latents to disk.  Every downstream job reads the cache, not the encoder. Cached layers
are $\{6,12,18,23\}$ out of 24; each clip yields an $8\times16\times16$ token grid (2048 tokens) per
layer.
\item \textbf{Decode.} A transformer video decoder with learned query tokens that cross-attend into
the frozen latent ``memory.'' It supports three modes: latent$\rightarrow$frame (reconstruct),
context$\rightarrow$future (predict), and latent$\rightarrow$state (regress physical variables).
\item \textbf{Probe.} Linear and MLP probes per layer, used to test \emph{what} is decodable and
\emph{how easily}.
\item \textbf{Steer.} Add a learned direction to the latents, re-decode, and sweep the step size
\alphanorm{} to see whether and how the output changes.
\item \textbf{Data.} Two sources. (a) Physics-IQ: real video clips, but with \emph{no} per-frame
numeric physics labels, so we can do reconstruction and category analysis but not exact state
regression. (b) A deterministic synthetic simulator with exact per-frame ground-truth state (position,
velocity, acceleration, gravity, collisions, visibility). The originally planned synthetic step was
rich 3D scenes (MuJoCo rigid bodies, Genesis fluids); Section~\ref{sec:next} argues for starting much
simpler than that.
\end{itemize}

\section{Step 0: does the decoder work at all? (\textcolor{blue}{RESULT: YES)}}
\label{sec:step0}
%==============================================================================

Before asking whether latents encode physics, we check the precondition: can anything be reconstructed
from the frozen latents? A 410M-parameter transformer decoder (24 layers, width 1024, 16 heads) was
trained for 20{,}000 steps on cached \vjepa{} Physics-IQ latents; training loss fell from 0.44 to
0.015.

\section{Step 1: category probe (\textcolor{blue}{RESULT: YES)}}
\label{sec:step1}
%==============================================================================

We ask whether the latent ``knows'' a coarse kind of physics: which physical \emph{regime} a clip
belongs to (solid mechanics vs.\ fluid dynamics vs.\ optics). We train a linear probe on
\emph{clip-pooled} \vjepa{}-large latents (the 2048 tokens for a clip are averaged into a single
$1024$-dim vector). Thermodynamics and magnetism are dropped because they have too few distinct
scenarios for a fair grouped split. Validation uses \textbf{scenario-grouped} cross-validation, so no
scenario appears in both train and test --- this is the right anti-leakage split for Physics-IQ.
Majority-class accuracy is $0.592$.

\paragraph{Is it just the picture being blue/bright?}
A natural worry: maybe the probe is exploiting global color (fluid clips look blue, etc.) rather than
physics. A pixel baseline using only the clip's mean RGB scores $0.490$ accuracy --- \emph{below} the
$0.592$ majority rate. So global brightness/color does not explain the latent result. (This rules out
the crudest appearance confound, not all of them; object identity and background are still entangled
with category, which becomes the crux in Step~3.)

\paragraph{Reading the result.}
Category structure is genuinely linear in the native latent space, and linear probes beat MLP probes at
every cached depth, which says the structure is simple, not something the probe has to bend itself into.
Accuracy climbs with depth, peaks in the upper-middle layers, and dips slightly at the very last layer;
because the peak layer differs by run (18 standardized, 23 raw), we should pick a steering layer from
the \emph{whole} depth curve rather than from a single argmax.

\paragraph{Geometry: decodable, but not tidy.}
The per-class mean directions (in raw latent space) are most anti-aligned for
fluid$\leftrightarrow$solid (cosine $-0.702$), then optics$\leftrightarrow$solid ($-0.482$), then
fluid$\leftrightarrow$optics ($-0.287$). So the fluid$\rightarrow$solid contrast is the cleanest axis to
attempt steering on. \emph{However}, the classes are not compact, well-separated blobs: the Fisher
ratio is $\approx 0.11$ and the silhouette score is $\approx 0.09$. In words, there exists a thin
discriminative \emph{direction} that classifies well even though the class point-clouds heavily overlap.
This is a foreshadowing: a direction good for \emph{classification} need not be a direction good for
\emph{steering}.

\section{Step 2: velocity-first, confound-controlled steering
    (\textcolor{blue}{RESULT: probe YES, occlusion YES, steering YES})}
  \label{sec:step2}
  %==============================================================================
  Velocity is the simplest dynamical quantity --- continuous, and in a simulator known exactly per
  frame --- so it is the cleanest possible target for the rung-5 test Step~3 failed. This phase builds
  the
  stripped dataset Step~3's negative argued for, and runs the probe$\rightarrow$steer chain on it. It is
  where the headline of these notes now sits: the decisive ``steer the latent and watch the pixels
  change at the right magnitude'' test, which Step~3 failed, \emph{passes} here.

  \paragraph{The bar we are setting.}
  Sonia's work shows ``velocity'' is decodable from \emph{some} subspace of the latents. We regard that
  as
  necessary but \textbf{not sufficient}. A probe that decodes velocity does not establish (a) that the
  probe direction is the subspace velocity actually \emph{lives} in, nor (b) that intervening on it
  changes the decoded velocity. The decisive test is the one Step~3 makes us respect: \emph{find the
  velocity direction, steer along it, decode, and confirm the ball's velocity actually changes} --- by
  re-probing the re-encoded decode \emph{and} by eye. Anything short of that is a classification result
  wearing a steering costume.

  \subsection{Design (each principle answers a Step-3 failure)}
  A 2D single ball on a clean white background --- no texture, palette, object-identity, or background,
  the
  exact confounds that let Step~3 ``succeed'' by repainting. Fixed FPS, so velocity is well-defined in
  pixels/frame; the ball stays fully in frame every frame, so velocity is never confounded with
  entry/exit. Per-frame metadata stores exact center $(x,y)$ and velocity $(v_x,v_y)$. Crucially, we keep
  time: the $2048\times1024$ latent is pooled spatially \emph{within each temporal slot} to an
  $8\times1024$ form and read with a \textbf{temporal} linear probe, rather than clip-mean-pooled to
  $1\times1024$ (the Step-1 limitation that discarded the time axis dynamics live in). Three variants:
  constant-velocity, occlusion (a static wall the ball passes behind), and rotated-but-same-speed.
  Spec: $32$ frames, $128\times128$, fixed FPS, white background. Demo clips were eyeballed first,
  dataset
  extracted second.

  \subsection{Probing results}

  \paragraph{Velocity probe (\textcolor{blue}{YES, strong}).}
  Velocity is almost perfectly linearly decodable. The temporal probe reaches $R^2\approx0.998$ across
  layers; even clip-pooled it is $R^2\approx0.996$, so the code survives pooling though the temporal form
  is consistently a touch better. Shuffled-label and random controls collapse to \emph{negative} $R^2$,
  so
  this is not leakage. This is the exact-regression version of the rung-2/3 test Step~1 could only run as
  a
  coarse category.

  \paragraph{Occlusion / object permanence (\textcolor{blue}{YES --- the headline probe}).}
  Train the probe on visible frames only, evaluate on the frames where the ball is \emph{fully hidden}
  behind the wall. Velocity stays decodable while the ball is invisible: $R^2\approx0.9975$ on hidden
  frames vs.\ $0.9986$ on visible --- essentially no drop. The latent maintains the physical state of an
  object it cannot see. This is the rung-4 result we flagged as the strongest available evidence that the
  representation is \emph{physical} rather than pixel-bound, and it held up cleanly.

  \paragraph{Equivariance (\textcolor{blue}{promising, not proven}).}
  Holding speed fixed and rotating the motion direction, the velocity subspace transforms approximately
  equivariantly: circularity $\approx0.95$, direction-$R^2\approx0.985$. Encouraging evidence that the
  subspace encodes velocity as a rotatable physical quantity rather than a dataset artifact, but the
  margins are not yet tight enough to call settled. Camera-viewpoint equivariance still needs a 3D
  renderer (\todo{choose 3D renderer for the viewpoint variant}).

  \subsection{Steering, take 1: the probe direction does not render}
  First attempt --- discover the velocity direction with the probe, push the latent along it, decode,
  and re-track the ball. The probe \emph{readout} is perfectly steerable: pushing along the velocity
  direction moves decoded-readout speed, $v_x$, and $v_y$ monotonically ($\rho=1.000$ each). But decode
  the steered latent and re-track the ball in pixels, and measured speed is essentially flat
  ($\sim\!0.009$ for every \alphanorm) against a ground-truth range of $0.008$--$0.038$. \textbf{Readout
  moves, pixels don't} --- the identical failure mode as Step-3 category steering. We root-caused and
  fixed three stacked decoder pathologies (sigmoid saturation on pure-white targets; foreground-gradient
  dilution; latent-blindness, where the decoder rendered a dataset-average ball), but even with the ball
  rendering, the decode would not track the steered \emph{magnitude}. The diagnosis: the probe direction
  is an \emph{abstract} axis fit to predict a number; pushing the latent along it goes \emph{off the
  manifold}, and the decoder will not faithfully render an off-manifold perturbation. This isolates the
  failure to the decoder/edit, not to the subspace --- which the probes show is genuinely there.

  \subsection{Steering, take 2: on-manifold difference vectors
      (\textcolor{blue}{YES --- the magnitude gap closes})}
  The fix has two halves, both motivated by ``stay on the manifold the decoder was trained on.''

  \emph{(i) The edit.} Following an LLM activation-steering analogy, derive the steering vector from
  \textbf{two real encoded clips of the same scene} that differ only in speed --- bit-identical first
  frame and direction. Let $H_a=E(\text{slow})$, $H_b=E(\text{fast})$, and $\Delta=H_b-H_a$. Decode
  $H_a+\alphanorm\!\cdot\!\Delta$: at $\alphanorm{=}0$ we are at the slow clip, at $\alphanorm{=}1$ the
  fast clip, and $\alphanorm\in[0,1]$ \emph{interpolates two reconstructable latents}, so the endpoints
  are guaranteed faithful and the path stays near the manifold.

  \emph{(ii) The decoder objective.} The remaining magnitude gap (the decoded ball $\sim$4$\times$ too
  slow even when reconstructing $H_b$) was the decoder's \emph{loss}, not its capacity: a pixel loss is
  minimized by painting a static, path-covering \emph{smear} whose centroid barely moves. We added a
  per-frame \textbf{frame-position loss} that ties the decoded ball's differentiable soft centroid to
  the ground-truth center every frame (a smear is then heavily penalized), plus a light anti-smear
  spread term.

  With both in place, the decisive test passes. On 20 held-out test scenes, decode the steered latent
  and re-track the ball in pixels:

  \textbf{This is the result Step~3 demanded and could not deliver}: steer the latent, decode, re-track
  in pixels, and the ball moves at the commanded speed --- visible by eye in the filmstrips, not just in
  a readout. \emph{Honest caveats.} Extrapolation outside $[0,1]$ \emph{saturates} (the decoded ball
  stops short of the ground-truth extrapolated speed --- expected, since it is off the trained velocity
  range). And a single \emph{global} averaged ``speed-up vector'' does \textbf{not} transfer ($\rho=0.30$
  on held-out scenes): the per-token $\Delta$ is localized to each scene's ball path, so averaging across
  scenes with different positions/directions blurs it. The per-pair, same-scene $\Delta$ is essential ---
  which raises the next question.

  \subsection{Toward a transferable velocity representation (2D velocity)}
  Per-pair $\Delta$ proves the counterfactual but needs a \emph{matched fast clip} for every edit. Is
  there instead a single, transferable velocity representation we can steer with, given only a desired
  velocity? To ask this properly we move from speed-only to true \textbf{2D velocity}
  (direction\,+\,magnitude): scenes of 8 clips share one start position, each clip a distinct velocity
  \emph{vector}. The headline metric becomes the angle error between the decoded and target velocity
  vectors (the ``stay-at-$v_a$'' no-op floor is $\approx44^\circ$, since the eight velocities differ by
  $\sim45^\circ$ in heading).

  Reading the table top to bottom. The per-pair full-$\Delta$ edit extends cleanly from speed to
  direction\,+\,magnitude (angle error $1.66^\circ$, speed ratio $0.997$): \textbf{faithful 2D velocity
  steering works} when we have the matched clip. A \emph{global, low-rank} velocity subspace $U$ is
  \textbf{real but partial}: $k{=}8$ components reach $21^\circ$ (vs.\ $44^\circ$ random) and saturate
  there. It cannot reach the per-pair $1.7^\circ$ because the velocity edit is not globally low-rank ---
  \emph{within} a scene the velocity $\Delta H$ has effective rank $\approx5.5$ (varying the
  \emph{direction} moves the ball through different tokens), and \emph{across} scenes the per-scene 2D
  velocity subspaces are near-orthogonal (mean principal angle $\sim71^\circ$). So $U$ captures the
  \emph{scene-shared} component of the edit ($\sim$37\%); the majority is scene-local \emph{placement}.
  A learned linear operator $F_U$ that maps a desired $\Delta v$ straight to a latent edit transfers to
  held-out scenes but only partially ($34.4^\circ$), exactly matching that 37\% ceiling. Two attempts to
  push past it are clean negatives: GT-informed start-roll \emph{canonicalization} gives no lift
  ($34.8^\circ$, because within a scene the start is already shared and the spread is direction-induced),
  and a \emph{direction-conditioned} binned operator makes generalization monotonically \emph{worse}
  ($40.3^\circ\rightarrow42.7^\circ$ as bins increase) --- splitting by heading discards the shared
  component without recovering the direction-specific one. \textbf{Takeaway:} the velocity counterfactual
  cannot be synthesized from the velocity \emph{command} alone by a (binned) linear operator; it is about
  token/path \emph{position}, so the per-pair edit (optionally denoised through the global $\sim$8D
  subspace) remains the faithful route. The open lever is a trajectory/position-conditioned operator,
  not more bins or scale.

  \subsection{Velocity is disentangled from appearance
      (\textcolor{blue}{YES})}
  A final worry: even a clean velocity edit is only safe if the velocity axis is \emph{distinct} from
  appearance --- otherwise ``steer faster'' might quietly recolor or resize the ball (the Step-3 disease).
  We test it directly. Build size, color, and background nuisance datasets; for each factor compute the
  top-8 PCA subspace of the same-scene $\Delta H$; then measure principal angles between the velocity
  subspace and each appearance-nuisance subspace. Calibration: two random 8D subspaces in this
  $\sim$2M-dimensional space sit at $89.9^\circ$ (``share nothing'').

  Velocity sits at $73$--$86^\circ$ from every appearance factor and climbs toward the $89.9^\circ$
  random reference in the deeper layers (L23 cleanest). Tellingly, the appearance factors overlap
  \emph{more with each other} than any does with velocity (size--color dips to $\sim60^\circ$ --- both
  are ball-token appearance edits). So the velocity axis is genuinely a distinct subspace: steering it
  should leave size/color/background largely untouched, with a small residual overlap that shrinks with
  depth. \emph{Honest caveat:} $73$--$86^\circ$ is not a perfect $90^\circ$ --- velocity shares a little
  with appearance, unsurprisingly, since both touch the ball's tokens.

  \paragraph{Where this leaves us.}
  Probe, occlusion, and equivariance establish that velocity actually \emph{lives in} --- and
  \emph{persists in} --- this subspace. The on-manifold difference-vector edit, paired with a
  frame-position decoder loss, then clears the bar Step~3 set: \textbf{steer the latent, decode, and the
  ball's pixel-measured velocity changes at the right magnitude} ($\rho=0.90$, $r=0.952$), in 2D
  ($1.7^\circ$ angle error), along an axis that is disentangled from appearance. The remaining honest
  gaps are about \emph{generality}, not existence: a faithful edit currently needs a matched same-scene
  clip (a global linear operator transfers only $\sim$37\% of it), and extrapolation beyond the trained
  velocity range saturates. The next lever is a trajectory/position-conditioned operator that could
  synthesize the scene-local part of the edit from geometry alone.

\section{Step 3: category steering (\textcolor{blue}{RESULT: NOT YET)}}
\label{sec:step3}
%==============================================================================

Here we attempt the top rung: take \emph{real fluid} Physics-IQ clips, push
their latents along the learned solid$-$fluid direction, decode, and sweep the step size \alphanorm{}
(measured as a fraction of a token's norm; token norms are $\sim$150--240). If the latents encoded a
manipulable notion of ``solid vs.\ fluid,'' we would expect the decoded liquid to start behaving like a
solid. It does not. The investigation went through three stages:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Probe-weight (max-margin) direction, deepest layer.} The probe \emph{readout} moves
perfectly monotonically with \alphanorm{} ($\rho=1.0$), but the \emph{pixels do not change} until the
decode simply collapses into mush at large \alphanorm{} ($\sim 0.85\times$norm). The discriminative
vector is real for classification but is not something the decoder can render --- exactly the
foreshadowing from Step~1's geometry.
\item \textbf{Difference-of-class-means direction, all layers.} Now the pixels \emph{do} move --- but
the effect is a global blue/cool \emph{appearance} shift plus block artifacts: the dataset's fluid
\emph{palette}, not its physics. And the probe readout \emph{saturates} at the smallest real
\alphanorm{} ($\geq 0.25$ already maxes out): a step function, not a controllability curve.
\item \textbf{Fine, low-\alphanorm{} sweep ($\leq 0.3$) across 6 distinct fluids} (balloon,
domino-in-juice, fill-glass, glass-stays-same, juice-in-water, liquid-on-duck). Every object and liquid
is \emph{preserved}; the only systematic effect is a mild blue background tint. Nothing freezes, holds
an edge, or solidifies.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Conclusion}
The solid/fluid axis is decodable as a \emph{label} and renderable as \emph{appearance}, but steering
along it \textbf{repaints the scene toward the target category's visual statistics rather than imposing
a physics counterfactual on a fixed object}. The crude category direction conflates appearance with
physics, and the decoder faithfully paints the appearance part. This is a \emph{mechanistic} negative:
it does not just say ``steering failed,'' it localizes \emph{why} --- the supervised category axis is
the wrong target because category is entangled with object identity, background, and palette. Step~2 is
the constructive answer to this negative: a single continuous quantity, in a confound-stripped dataset,
\emph{is} steerable.

\paragraph{What the negative tells us to do next.}
Two design faults caused it, and both are fixable: (i) the \emph{target variable} (a discrete category)
is entangled with appearance, and (ii) the \emph{data} (rich, varied real clips) is full of confounds.
The next phase removes both: pick a single, continuous, exactly-known physical variable, and put it in a
dataset stripped of everything else.

\section{Next steps}
\label{sec:next}
%==============================================================================

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Transfer the velocity edit to real video.} The Step-2 edit is proven on synthetic 2D balls;
the open question is whether a velocity direction learned on synthetic data steers velocity in real
Physics-IQ clips through the same decoder. This is the quantity-direction redo of the Step-3 negative.
\item \textbf{A position/trajectory-conditioned operator} to recover the scene-local part of the velocity
